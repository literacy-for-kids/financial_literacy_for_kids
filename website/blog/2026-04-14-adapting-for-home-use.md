# Adapting This Curriculum for Home Use

This curriculum was designed to work in classrooms, homeschool settings, after-school programs, and around the kitchen table. But if you are a parent or caregiver using it at home, you might be wondering: *"How do I make this work with just one or two kids?"*

Here are some practical tips.

<!-- truncate -->

## You Do Not Need a Classroom

Every activity in this curriculum has a "For Facilitators" box with multiple variations — including versions for small groups and one-on-one settings. If you see an activity that says "have students pair up," you can be the partner. If it says "discuss as a class," you can have a conversation over dinner.

The learning happens through **thinking and talking**, not through group size.

## Pick Your Pace

The curriculum is organized into 18 weeks with three sessions per week, each about 20 minutes. But you do not have to follow that schedule. Some approaches that work well at home:

- **One session per week:** Spread the curriculum across a school year. Do one 20-minute session each week and let the ideas simmer between sessions.
- **Two sessions per week:** A comfortable pace that gives learners time to absorb concepts without rushing.
- **Binge mode:** If your learner is excited, do multiple sessions in a day. Just watch for signs of fatigue — the quality of thinking matters more than the quantity of content covered.

## Use Everyday Moments

The best financial literacy teaching at home happens **outside the official sessions**:

- At the grocery store: "We have $50 for groceries. What should we prioritize?"
- At a restaurant: "If you have $10 for lunch, what are your options? What is the tradeoff?"
- Receiving allowance or gift money: "How would you budget this? What categories would you create?"
- Seeing an ad: "What is this ad trying to get you to do? How does it work?"

These moments reinforce the curriculum's concepts in real life — which is where financial literacy ultimately lives.

## Handling Tough Questions

Kids at home often feel more comfortable asking direct questions than kids in a classroom. You might hear:

- *"Are we rich or poor?"*
- *"Why can't we afford that?"*
- *"How much money do you make?"*

These are natural, healthy questions. You do not have to answer with specific numbers if you are not comfortable. Instead, try:

- "We have enough for what we need, and we make choices about the rest. That is what budgeting is."
- "That is something we are choosing not to spend on right now. Can you think of why?"
- "I earn enough to take care of our family. The exact amount is private, but I am happy to talk about how we make spending decisions."

The curriculum gives learners the **vocabulary and mental models** to have these conversations. Your honesty and willingness to engage — even without exact numbers — is what makes the learning stick.

## You Are the Best Facilitator

You know your learner better than any curriculum designer ever could. If a lesson does not resonate, skip it and come back later. If your learner is fascinated by one topic, spend extra time on it. The age adaptation notes on each weekly page can help you calibrate.

Trust your instincts. If you are talking about money in a curious, non-judgmental way — you are already doing it right.

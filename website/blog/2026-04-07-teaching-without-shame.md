# Teaching Financial Literacy Without Shame

One of the most important decisions we made when building this curriculum was a simple one: **never make kids feel bad about money**.

That might sound obvious, but it is surprisingly hard to do. Money is tangled up with identity, class, family stress, and social comparison. When a lesson asks "How much does your family spend on groceries?" or "What do your parents do for work?" — even with good intentions — it can land differently for a child whose family is struggling.

Here is how we approach it differently.

<!-- truncate -->

## The Problem With "Real Numbers"

Many financial literacy programs ask kids to work with their family's actual finances. The idea is that real numbers make lessons more relevant. And sometimes they do. But they also create problems:

- A child whose family cannot afford extras may feel embarrassed during a budgeting exercise.
- A child whose family is wealthy may feel awkward or draw unwanted attention.
- Some families consider financial details private — and that boundary deserves respect.

In this curriculum, **every activity uses fictional scenarios and play money.** Learners practice the same skills — budgeting, tradeoffs, resource planning — without anyone needing to reveal personal information.

## Language Matters

We avoid phrases like:
- "Can your family afford...?"
- "If your parents make $X..."
- "Rich people do this, poor people do that..."

Instead, we use neutral framing:
- "Imagine a family that earns $200 a week..."
- "If you had $20 to split between three categories..."
- "What would you choose, and what would you give up?"

This keeps the focus on **the thinking process**, not on anyone's circumstances.

## Curiosity Over Judgment

Every lesson in this curriculum is built around curiosity: "What do you notice?" rather than "You should do this." We never tell learners that spending is bad, saving is always good, or borrowing is wrong. Instead, we help them see tradeoffs and make informed choices.

The goal is a learner who thinks: *"I understand what is happening with this money, and I can make a thoughtful choice"* — not one who feels guilty every time they buy something.

## For Facilitators

If you are teaching this curriculum, here are a few practical tips:

1. **Never ask about family finances publicly.** If a learner volunteers information, that is their choice. But do not put them on the spot.
2. **Normalize all budget levels.** When discussing examples, vary the amounts. Some families have more, some have less. Both are normal.
3. **Watch for shame signals.** If a learner withdraws during a money discussion, they may be feeling self-conscious. Shift to a fictional scenario.
4. **Celebrate thoughtful choices, not big numbers.** A learner who carefully allocates a $10 budget is demonstrating the same skill as one who allocates $100.

Financial literacy is a superpower. But only if the learner feels safe enough to engage with it.
